One Free Miracle

The Emergence of Life from a Neo-Darwinian Perspective



10th Dec. 2023

In the book The Science Delusion (2013) Rupert Sheldrake refers to a conversation he had with Terence McKenna in 1998 [1]. Sheldrake and McKenna are counter-culture figures, with Sheldrake's book positioning science as fraught with dogmas of its own, and McKenna contributing the Stoned-Ape Theory of Human Evolution, among other things. He quotes McKenna as saying that science proceeds on the granting of "one free miracle", which thereafter unfolds with a "seamless, causal explanation". Suffice to say, the material explanations which scientific orthodoxy posits sit uncomfortably with these two men. In particular, Sheldrake takes great effort to criticise the Gene-Centered view of the world, popularised in The Selfish Gene by Richard Dawkins (1976).

In this article, I want to explore the Gene-Centered view of the world, or more aptly, Neo-Darwinism in the context of the "one free miracle". I adore the phrase "one free miracle" because it succinctly captures the apparent absurdity of existence. Yet, Neo-Darwinism, despite Sheldrakes protestations, provides a powerful, consoling, and somewhat simple explanation to the problem (albeit still incomplete).

Beyond just explaining it abstractly, I would like to demonstrate the explanation so it unfolds as naturally as it has done in reality. To facilitate such an understanding, I will create simulations using Python, allowing you to verify the concepts for yourself. This is similar to work created by Primer on Youtube, which I highly recommend.

Something Vs. Nothing

We can think of existence as being a dichotomous phenomenon; something can either exist (ùëÜ), or not exist (ùëÅ ). Indeed, the set of all possible objects appears to be infinite, yet there is only one way of not existing. Hence, if one wants to put a probability on existence, it is simply

$$ P(S) = \infty, P(N) = \dfrac{1}{\infty} $$

Of course, this is not a legitimate way of describing the probability of any one thing in particular. Indeed, a single object has the same probability of existing as not existing if randomly sampled from the universal set i.e. exceedingly small ( $\tfrac{1}{\infty}$ ).

This is the argument known as Hoyle's Fallacy which equates the emergence of life from non-living matter as being "comparable to the chance that a tornado sweeping through a junkyard might assemble a Boeing 747 from the materials therein". It is precisely this argument which we will see to be false.

Life as it is best understood exists by being born, and ceases to exist by dying. If we consider Hoyle's proposal of random chance we can imagine a universe where simple life is spawned into existence.
