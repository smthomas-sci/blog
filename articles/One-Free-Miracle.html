One Free Miracle

The Emergence of Life from a Neo-Darwinian Perspective



12th June. 2020

In the book The Science Delusion (2013) Rupert Sheldrake refers to a conversation he had with Terence McKenna in 1998 [1]. Sheldrake and McKenna are counter-culture figures, with Sheldrake's book positioning science as fraught with dogmas of its own, and McKenna contributing the Stoned-Ape Theory of Human Evolution, among other things. He quotes McKenna as saying that science proceeds on the granting of "one free miracle", which thereafter unfolds with a "seamless, causal explanation". Suffice to say, the material explanations which scientific orthodoxy posits sit uncomfortably with these two men. In particular, Sheldrake takes great effort to criticise the Gene-Centered view of the world, popularised in The Selfish Gene by Richard Dawkins (1976).

In this article, I want to explore the Gene-Centered view of the world, or more aptly, Neo-Darwinism in the context of the "one free miracle". I adore the phrase "one free miracle" because it succinctly captures the apparent absurdity of existence. Yet, Neo-Darwinism, despite Sheldrakes protestations, provides a powerful, consoling, and somewhat simple explanation to the problem (albeit still incomplete).

Beyond just explaining it abstractly, I would like to demonstrate the explanation so it unfolds as naturally as it has done in reality. To facilitate such an understanding, I will create simulations using Python, allowing you to verify the concepts for yourself. This is similar to work created by Primer on Youtube, which I highly recommend.

Something Vs. Nothing

We can think of existence as being a dichotomous phenomenon; something can either exist (ùëÜ), or not exist (ùëÅ ). Indeed, the set of all possible objects appears to be infinite, yet there is only one way of not existing. Hence, if one wants to put a probability on existence, it is simply

$$ P(S) = \infty, P(N) = \dfrac{1}{\infty} $$

Of course, this is not a legitimate way of describing the probability of any one thing in particular. Indeed, a single object has the same probability of existing as not existing if randomly sampled from the universal set i.e. exceedingly small ( $\tfrac{1}{\infty}$ ).

This is the argument known as Hoyle's Fallacy which equates the emergence of life from non-living matter as being "comparable to the chance that a tornado sweeping through a junkyard might assemble a Boeing 747 from the materials therein". It is precisely this argument which we will see to be false.

Life as it is best understood exists by being born, and ceases to exist by dying. If we consider Hoyle's proposal of random chance we can imagine a universe where simple life is spawned into existence. Such a life form has a chance of being born, or a birth rate $B$ , and a chance of dying, a death rate $D$. We will represent this as follows: 


class Replicator:
    """
    A simple life form called a replicator.

    Upon creation [ Replicator() ], the replicator has 
    a chance of being born according to its birth_rate, otherwise
    it is dead (self.alive = False)
    """

    def __init__(self, birth_rate: float, death_rate: float):
        self.B = birth_rate
        self.D = death_rate
        self.alive = True if random.random() <= self.B else False
        
    def step(self):
        """
        If replicator is alive, simulate chance of death.
        """
        self.alive = False if random.random() <= self.D and self.alive else self.alive

We can now create a replicator where $B=0.8$ and $D=0.2$, and then check if it is alive. 


    replicator = Replicator(0.8, 0.2)
    print(replicator.alive)
    --
    True

Life exists through time and so we can move to the next point in time using the step() method. This then simulates the chance of death at that time step according to $D$. 


    replicator.step()
    print(replicator.alive)
    --
    True

We can simulate this over any number of steps to understand the replicators life-span.

We can see that most of them eventually die. However, we also have to include the fact that the universe spontaneously creates more replicators according to $B$. If we include this step, by adding to the population progressively and running the simulation much longer something interesting is revealed. 


N = 1
population = [ Replicator(birth_rate=0.8, death_rate=0.2) for n in range(N)]
tally = []

            population.remove(r)

We can see that the population grows and then shrinks, apparently oscilating around an equilibrium. To understand what is happening, let's considering the maths. If $B=D$, then the population would stay the same, because for every birth there is an equal chance of death. However, if they are different, then the number that can die depends on the number that are alive, $N$. $$ B=N \times D $$ This enables us to estimate the equilibrium by taking the ratio between the birth and death rates. $$ N=BD $$ 


    B = 0.8
    D = 0.2
    N = B/D
    print(N)
    --
    4.0

Knowing this, we can predict the equilibrium population for a variety of different birth and death rates. The results show that the simulations behave according to theory, oscilating around their respective equilibria. e.g. $N = B / D = 0.9 / 0.02 = 45$.

Valid values to experiment with are between $0$ and $1$ .If you explore sufficiently you will discover something fundamental. It doesn't matter how small a chance there is of being born, as long as it is higher than the death rate. The only thing that matters is the ratio. We can summarise two important discoveries as follows.

  1. Looking at individuals is unhelpful, and so a universe must spontaneously produce simple replicators to create a population. Importantly, a replicator at this stage need not refer to life as we understand it in its most complex form. It is simply an abstraction that we will explore in coming sections.
  2. It is the ratio between the chance of being born and the chance of dying that allows populations to grow, however slight the chances are of that population getting started. The only thing required under the most unlikely of circustances is time.
With enough time (~14 billion years for our own universe) theory tells us that life is an inevitability.

Escape Velocity

The equilibrium for a given pair of birth and death rates appears to limit the size of the population. For $B=0.9$ and $D=0.01$, then the population is limited to $90$. You might wonder then, how does a population grow past that? $N$ scales inversely to $D$ and so if we decrease $D$ by another order of magnitude, we get $$ N = \dfrac{B}{D} = \dfrac{0.9}{0.001} = 900 $$ We can increase $N$ simply by making the chance of death exceedingly rare. However, in doing so, the probability of having such a combination of $B$ and $D$ also becomes exceedingly rare. Below is the full space of combinations. The combinations with large population sizes occupy only a small portion of the full space. Yet, even if such a lucky combination is found $N$ is still bounded by the equilibrium. How does life get past this limit?

A clue comes from prexisting prokaryotic life, namely Bacteria. Populations of Bacteria increase because individuals repliciate(hence the use of the term Replicator!). If we assume that a single Bacertia is "born", within an environment is would have a death rate $D$ and a replication rate $R$. By replicating, a copy of itself is added to the population. We can update the Replicator class to include a replication rate and an ability to replicate. 


class Replicator:
    """
    A simple life form called a replicator.
    
    Upon creation the replicator has 
    a change of being born according to its birth_rate, otherwise
    it is dead (self.alive = False)
    """
    
    
    def __init__(self, birth_rate:float, death_rate:float, replication_rate:float):
        self.B = birth_rate
        self.D = death_rate
        self.R = replication_rate
        self.alive = True if random.random() <= self.B else False
        
    def replicate(self):
        """
        Creates a copy of itself.
        """
        return Replicator(self.B, self.D, self.R)
        
    def step(self):
        """
        If replicator is alive, simulate chance of replication and then death
        
        Returns offspring
        """
        offspring = self.replicate() if random.random() <= self.R and self.alive else None
        
        self.alive = False if random.random() <= self.D and self.alive else self.alive
        
        return offspring

Given that replicators can now replicate, the combination of dying and replicating is going to effect the population. The number dying and is offset, or reduced by the number replicating. We can represent this as: $$ (D \times N ) - (R \times N) = N(D-R) $$ We can then substitute this into our original model: $$ N = \dfrac{B}{D-R} $$ However, the spontaenous birth rate must be zero, because the replicator cannot pop into existence without a parent. Therefore, the equation isn't helpful to us because $N$ is always $0$. A way past this is to forget about a birth rate of $0$ and change perspectives...

Thinking back to what was said in the first section, we know that if $B=D$, the population stays the same. When they are different then the population changes, either increasing or decreasing. We can write the expected change $\Delta$ as: $$ \Delta = B - D $$ We can include N to calculate the exact numbers for a given population. $$ \Delta = B - (D \times N) $$ But importantly, we now need to include the way replication increases the population. $$ \Delta = (B+R \times N) - (D \times N) = B + (R-D) \times N $$ This means that for any number of creatures, we can predict the change in the number. To begin, let's choose $B=1$, $D=0.2$ and $R=0$, so that no replication occurs. The change will be calculated for a range of $N=(0,10) $. 


N = [ float(n) for n in range(11) ]
B = 1
R = 0
D = 0.2
Delta = [B + (R - D)*n for n in N]

The orange dot shows us the equilibrium point, where if the population goes below $5$, the expected change goes up, and if it goes above $5$, then the expected change goes down. Now let's make $R=0.1$.

In this case, since the replication rate increased, our equilibrium changed to $10$. If we run several simulations under these conditions we will see that the equilibrium is reached.

If the replication rate equals the death rate then we have linear growth, always increasing by the birth rate.

It becomes more interesting when the replication rate $R=0.2$ is higher than the death rate $D=0.1$.

We can now finally see how replication can lead to exponential growth. We have escaped the equilibrium point and the population is increasing! However, we have only considered when $B=1$, which guarentees spontaneous generation at each time step. What happens when populations depend only on replication?

Over the course of each of these simulations, if the population ever reaches $0$ than they get stuck - this is called extinction. Life that depends only on replication survives by walking a very thing line. How much they are at risk of extinction depends on sensitive difference between $R$ and $ùê∑$. In this case, 42% of the 200 simulated populations went extinct.

We have so far depended on our "one free miracle" with a starting population of at least $1$. This leaves us with an interesting predictament. If complex organisms always need a parent, but they can't replicate unless they already exist, how do they get started in the first place?

Emergence of Complex Life

All life on Earth utilies a genetic code, based on either ribonucleic acid (RNA), or deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA). It is theorised that early life emerged from RNA, a code which has four symbols: A, U, G, C. The symbols strung together form sequences such as:

  • GCGGAA
  • AAAAUU
  • AUCGAC

What sequences we see in living organisms today are not random. However, we can simplify the scenario to say that the simplest lifeform in our simulated universe has a sequence of just A. Being incredibly simple, they spontenously emerge from the environment. Once alive they can also replicate or die. However, when they undergo replication, there is a chance $M$, that their offspring will be a mutant with a genetic code of U. This new genetic variant turns out not to be very good, and in fact has a higher death rate than its parent.

We can update the Replicator class to include the RNA code, and see what happens when we run a simulation with the following parameters.

  • A: $B=1, D=0.5, R=0, M=0.01$
  • U: $B=0, D=0.8, R=0.7, M=0$ 


class Replicator:
    """
    A simple life form called a replicator.
    
    Upon creation the replicator has 
    a change of being born according to its birth_rate, otherwise
    it is dead (self.alive = False)
    """
    def __init__(self, birth_rate, death_rate, replication_rate, mutation_rate, genetic_code, alive=False):
        self.B = birth_rate
        self.D = death_rate
        self.R = replication_rate
        self.M = mutation_rate
        self.genes = genetic_code
        self.alive = alive
        # Spontaneous birth
        if not self.alive:
            self.alive = True if random.random() <= self.B else False
        
    def replicate(self, rules):
        """
        Replicates according to what the rules dictate for its genes.
        """
        return Replicator(0, self.D, self.R, self.M, self.genes, True)
    
    def mutate(self, rules):
        """
        Mutates a replicator according to the rules
        """
        self.genes = rules.get_mutant(self) if np.random.random() <= self.M else self.genes
        # Get parameters for genotype
        params = rules.get_params(self.genes)
        # Update replicator
        self.B = params["B"]
        self.D = params["D"]
        self.R = params["R"]
        self.M = params["M"]
        
    def step(self, rules):
        """
        If replicator is alive, simulate chance of replication and then death
        
        Returns offspring
        """
        # Environmental mutation
        self.mutate(rules)
        
        # Replicate 
        offspring = self.replicate(rules) if random.random() <= self.R and self.alive else None
        
        # Risk death!
        self.alive = False if random.random() <= self.D and self.alive else self.alive
        
        return offspring
    

class Rules:
    """
    Contains the rules of the Universe
    """
    def __init__(self):
        self.transitions = { "A" : "U", "U" : None, "G" : None, "C" : None }
    
        self.specifications = {
            "A" : {"B" : 1, "D": 0.5, "R" : 0, "M": 0.01, "alive": True},
            "U" : {"B" : 0, "D": 0.8, "R" : 0.7, "M": 0, "alive": True}
        }
        
    def get_mutant(self, replicator):
        return self.transitions[replicator.genes]
        
    def get_params(self, gene):
        return self.specifications[gene]

This shows us that a steady stream of simple replicators of type A will reach an equilibrium. Yet from that population, every so often one will mutate to U, which despite it's chance of death, gains an ability to replicate. Of course, it's chance of death is greater and so it inevitably tends towards extinction. It just wasn't a good mutation. Let's look at a new equation to incorporate the mutation. $$ \Delta = B + (R (1-M)-D) \times N $$ We modify the replication value, by saying that if 1% are going to mutate, then 99% will replicate as usual, hence $R(1‚àíM)$. Now if we multiply out the equation an interesting term pops out. $$ \Delta = B + RN - RMN - DN $$ It is $RMN$, which is the total number of A that mutate to U. We can use this term to keep track of the number of each type, by adding it to it's expected popuation equation as follows: $$ \Delta A = B_{1} + (R_{1}(1-M_{1}) - D_{1}) \times N_{1} $$ $$ \Delta U = B_{2} + (R_{2}(1-M_{2}) - D_{2}) \times N_{2} + R_{1}M_{1}N_{1} $$ Let's now simulate a universe where many mutations are possible. After running the simulation for 250 steps we experience a population explosion. The C begins growing exponentially once emerging after only a couple of G appear. We can see that from the constant production of basic replicators ( A), we can mutate towards something that has exponential growth. In this way, we have shown that we can generate complexity and in large numbers by building on top of the most basic replicator. Even from a succession of bad mutations (U, G), we have seen that we can eventually get exponential replicators which, with the environment permitting, could later result in even more interesting mutations.

This is an enormous discovery; it tells us that no miracle is necessary! We only have to consider the simplest replicator in the environment, so simple that the environment naturally produces it. If the environment allows variation by way of mutation, and a particular mutation confers the ability to replicate, then under such conditions life is an inevitabilty.

Survival of the Fittest

Terminology

Existence - The dichotomy of either being alive or dead.

Neo-Darwinism - A theory of evolution by natural selection where by the unit of selection is a gene within a population.

Replicator - The most basic form of life whose structure allows it to make (imperfect) copies of itself.

Population - A collection of genes upon which selection takes place.

Equilibrium - The fixed point around which population numbers fluxuate.

Birth:Death Ratio - The ratio between the rates that molecular structures come in and out of existence

Mutation - A spontaneous structural change within a molecular structure or replicator which confers an existantial advantage or disadvantage.

Replication - the process available to replicators that allows their population to grow exponentially (if the replication rate is greater than the death rate)

Extinction - refers to the permanent demise of a population of replicators whose death rate exceeds their replication rate.

Scarcity - the resource limiations imposed on replicators by their environment (includes other replicators), denying the possibility of infinite growth and therefore leading to competition for those resources.

Carrying Capacity - the total number replicators that an environment can sustain (theoretically, but impossible to calculate in practice)

Selection - The process where replicators with disadvantagous mutations are removed from the population through death.

Adapation - The process where replicators accumulate mutations well-suited (tentatively) to survival in the environment

Fitness - the competative capacity of a replicator to replicate.

Summary

The contended "one free miracle" is not necessary to explain our seemingly improbable circumstance. In a universe of abundant elementary molecules, we see the spontaneous process of chemical reactions manfiest the entrance and exit of existence. This is hardly improbable given it is the natural order of things. Given enough time, mutations serendipitously lead to molecular units which can make copies of themselves. At this point we've reached escape velovity, with exponential growth transforming the world, not by degree but kind. Of course, the peristant problem of imperfect replication allows for differential advantage to be found at every turn, with equal risk of surival or extinction. In pockets of the environment where resources are limited, competition ensues, where by replicators are increasingly adapted to the task of replication. Despite this process elegantly explaining the abundant and diverse presence of life on our planet, it is critical to realise evolution operates only in hindsite. There is no direction, no up towards which it aims. Despite this, we are witness to a beautiful creative force on the world by which physics is the only limiation to its ingenuity. More impressively, we understand it and now see that evolution is just as formormidable as all other forces of nature.
